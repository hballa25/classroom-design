One Place to Run It All
Here’s where it all comes together. By now you’ve got a shelf of agents that each do one job well — a grading assistant here, a planning partner there, a tutor for the kids. Useful, but scattered. They don’t talk to each other, your notes live in six places, and YOU are still the wire connecting everything. The Command Center fixes that. It’s one hub where your agents, your notes, your plans, and your data all live together and feed each other — so your whole teaching life runs from a single place instead of seventeen browser tabs and a sticky-note avalanche.
Big honest caveat up front, because I respect you too much to oversell: this is a BLUEPRINT, not an app you download. You’re not buying software and you’re not maintaining code. You’re assembling a system out of tools that already exist — a notes app as the hub, the agents you already built, and a little light automation — wired together the way I wired mine. No app store, no subscriptions to a thing I’m selling, nothing to break at 11 p.m. before observations. Just a system you stand up once and run.
I built mine around Obsidian, and that’s what I’ll walk you through, because it’s free, it’s yours (your notes live on your own device, not someone’s server), and it connects to everything. But the CONCEPTS work in whatever you already use — Notion, OneNote, Google Docs, even a well-organized Drive. I’ll flag the alternatives as we go. Don’t let the specific app be the thing that stops you.
Fair warning, same as the agent book: this is the advanced one. I’m assuming you’ve built at least a couple of agents and you’re comfortable. If ‘wire your agents into a hub’ sounds like sci-fi, go build a Gem or two first and come back. This is the capstone, and it’s worth doing when you’re ready for it.

Layer 1 — The Hub (Your Vault)
What it is. The hub is your home base — one place where everything about your teaching lives and links together. In Obsidian this is a ‘vault’: a folder of notes you control, that link to each other like a personal wiki for your classroom.
Why it matters. Right now your teaching life is scattered — plans in Drive, notes on paper, ideas in your head, data in a spreadsheet you forget exists. A hub ends the scatter. Everything has one home, and because notes link to each other, finding ‘that thing I wrote about the cells unit’ takes three seconds instead of a tab-by-tab archaeology dig.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
I resisted the ‘build a second brain’ thing for a long time because it sounded like a productivity-bro rabbit hole, and I did NOT have time to become a note-taking hobbyist. What changed my mind was realizing my teaching life was genuinely scattered across Drive, paper, my actual brain, and a spreadsheet I forgot existed until report cards. The hub fixed the scatter, not by being fancy, but by giving everything ONE home. My honest advice: build it small. Four folders and a dashboard. I overbuilt mine the first time, made it gorgeous, and never used it because it was too precious to touch. Ugly and used beats beautiful and abandoned.
Build It
Install Obsidian (free) and create one vault called something like ‘Teaching HQ.’
Make four folders to start: Units, Lessons, Students (no sensitive data — notes and patterns only), and Agents.
Create one ‘Dashboard’ note as your front door — links to your current unit, this week’s plans, and your agent list.
Don’t over-build it. Four folders and a dashboard. You’ll grow it as you actually use it.
⇄ NOT USING OBSIDIAN? — the concept works anywhere
No Obsidian? A Notion workspace, a OneNote notebook, or even a single well-structured Google Doc with links can be your hub. The rule is the same: ONE home, everything links back to it.

layer 2 ✎
Layer 2 — Capture (Get It All In One Place)
What it is. This layer is about funneling everything — lesson ideas, student observations, plans, that thought you had in the shower — into the hub instead of losing it. Quick capture, consistent home.
Why it matters. The best system in the world fails if stuff never makes it in. Teachers lose brilliant ideas constantly because they happen between classes with no time to record them. A frictionless capture habit means your hub actually reflects your teaching instead of being a pretty, empty folder.
Build It
Set up a single ‘Inbox’ note (or use Obsidian’s daily note) where anything gets dumped fast, sorted later.
Use templates for the things you make often — a lesson template, a unit template — so capture is fill-in-the-blank, not blank-page.
Pick ONE capture habit and keep it: end of each class, jot one line about what worked or bombed.
Weekly, spend ten minutes moving inbox items to their real folders. That’s the whole maintenance cost.
⇄ NOT USING OBSIDIAN? — the concept works anywhere
Any notes app with a quick-capture (Apple Notes, Google Keep, Notion quick-add) works. The point is low friction — if capturing takes more than a few seconds, you won’t do it.

Layer 3 — Wire In Your Agents
What it is. This is where the Command Center earns its name: connecting the agents you built in the last book to your hub, so they work FROM your real materials and their outputs land back in one place.
Why it matters. An agent floating in a browser tab is useful. An agent connected to your hub — pulling from your real unit notes, dropping its output into your real lesson folder — is a system. This is the difference between ‘I have some AI tools’ and ‘my classroom runs on a connected machine.’
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
This is the moment the whole thing stopped being ‘some AI tricks’ and started being a system. The shift was small but it rewired how my days feel: instead of hunting for my rubric across downloads every time I graded, it just lives in my vault and the grading assistant pulls from it. Instead of the tutor’s summaries vanishing into a chat history, they land in the kid’s note where I’ll actually see them. Nothing dramatic — just everything finally being in one place and feeding each other. The first week it clicked, I remember thinking I’d accidentally built myself a little teaching co-pilot out of free tools.
Build It
In your Agents folder, make one note per agent: what it does, its instructions (so you can rebuild or share it), and a link to open it.
Feed your agents from the hub: when your Grading Assistant needs your rubric, it’s right there in your vault, ready to paste or upload.
Land outputs back home: paste the planning partner’s drafts into your Lessons folder, the tutor’s session summaries into the student’s note.
Now your hub is the agents’ memory — the context they need lives in one place you control.
⇄ NOT USING OBSIDIAN? — the concept works anywhere
This works regardless of hub — the principle is that your agents’ inputs (rubrics, standards) and outputs (drafts, summaries) both live in your hub, not scattered across downloads and chat histories.

Layer 4 — The Automation Layer (Light Touch)
What it is. A little automation that moves information around without you doing it by hand — connecting your tools so things flow. Keep it light: a few high-value connections, not a fragile Rube Goldberg machine.
Why it matters. This is the layer that buys back real time, but it’s also where people overengineer and build something that breaks constantly. The honest move is to automate only the boring, repetitive hand-offs — and leave the rest manual. A few good automations beat fifty brittle ones you’re always fixing.
Build It
Start with ONE automation that solves a real annoyance — e.g., a form response that auto-lands in a tracking sheet your agent reads.
Use no-code connectors (your school’s Google tools, simple Zapier/Make flows) — nothing that requires actual coding.
Test it, then leave it alone. Automation you have to babysit isn’t saving you time.
Resist the urge to automate everything. If a task takes 30 seconds and happens rarely, just do it by hand.
⇄ NOT USING OBSIDIAN? — the concept works anywhere
No appetite for automation? Skip this layer entirely. The Command Center works fully as a manual system — automation is the bonus, not the foundation. Many teachers stop at Layer 3 and are thrilled.

Layer 5 — The Operating Rhythm
What it is. The system only works if you actually run it. This layer is the light weekly and daily rhythm that keeps the Command Center alive instead of becoming another abandoned app.
Why it matters. Every teacher has a graveyard of systems they set up once and never touched again. The difference between a tool you use and a tool you abandon is a tiny, sustainable rhythm — not a heroic daily ritual. This is how the Command Center becomes a habit instead of a guilt object.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Here’s my graveyard confession: I have set up SO many systems I never touched again. Planners, apps, color-coded everything — abandoned by October. What finally made one stick was making the upkeep almost insultingly small. Two minutes a day, fifteen on the weekend. The second a system asks more than that, real teaching life steamrolls it. So when I tell you to keep the rhythm tiny, it’s not me being nice — it’s the only version that survives contact with a normal teaching week. Don’t build a system that needs a heroic version of you to run it. Build one that the tired, normal you will actually keep.
Build It
Daily (2 min): open your dashboard, glance at today, jot one capture note after your classes.
Weekly (15 min): clear your inbox into folders, check your mastery data, set up next week from your templates.
Monthly (20 min): refresh your agents’ uploaded content for the new unit, prune notes you don’t need.
That’s it. Under 20 minutes a week of upkeep to run your entire teaching life from one place.
⇄ NOT USING OBSIDIAN? — the concept works anywhere
Whatever your hub, the rhythm is the same: a tiny daily touch, a short weekly tidy, a monthly refresh. Keep it small enough that you’ll actually do it.
A Day in the Life (Once It's Running)
Let me show you what a normal day actually looks like once this is running, because ‘connected system’ is abstract until you see it in motion.
Morning, coffee in hand: I open my dashboard note. It links to today’s lesson, which my planning partner drafted from the standards already sitting in my vault. The Do Now’s ready because yesterday’s exit-slip data — captured into the hub — told the loop what to review. I skim, tweak one thing, done. Five minutes.
During the day: a kid says something brilliant in third period, so I dump one line into my inbox note between classes. After my last class, I jot what worked and what bombed — ten seconds, and it’s captured forever instead of forgotten by the parking lot.
After school: the grading assistant drafts feedback off the rubric that lives in my vault; I review and personalize. The tutor’s session summaries from last night’s ‘homework’ are sitting in my students’ notes, so I already know who practiced and who’s stuck before I even plan tomorrow. The whole loop closes, and I’m out the door at a reasonable hour.
None of that is sci-fi. It’s five free-or-cheap tools, wired together once, run with a 2-minute daily habit. That’s the Command Center. You already built the parts in the last three books — this just connects them into the machine.
