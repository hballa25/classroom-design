I'm a working teacher. I built this for myself first, because I was drowning in the same busywork you are — the warm-ups, the exit slips, the grading that follows you home. Then AI got good enough to actually help, and this book is that system, cleaned up so you can steal it.

Mastery Loop — Step 1
workflow 1 ✎
WORKFLOW 1
Do Now From Yesterday's Lesson
The time drain. It's 7:38 a.m. The bell's in twelve minutes and you're staring at a blank slide trying to invent a warm-up that isn't 'copy the objective.' Do that five times a day until June and you've basically donated a work week to bell-ringers nobody remembers.
Why bother. You already know retrieval practice works — pulling stuff OUT of their heads beats letting them passively re-read it. I'm not going to make you sit through the cognitive-science TED talk. The move here is dead simple: build a 5-minute warm-up straight from yesterday's lesson, in about the time it takes your coffee to cool, so the first five minutes of class actually does something.
The mastery angle. Bonus you'll feel immediately: the second they start scribbling, you can see who actually kept yesterday's lesson and who let it evaporate on the bus ride home. That's free diagnostic data before you've even said good morning.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
I used to think a Do Now just had to exist. Throw something on the board, buy myself five minutes to take attendance, done. Then I actually watched what the good ones did — a warm-up that connects to something they already cared about gets them locked in before they’ve even taken their hoodie off. In Living Environment I started opening with a quick callback to whatever weird thing hooked them yesterday (the kid who would NOT let go of how sharks don’t get cancer — that energy), and suddenly the first five minutes stopped being crowd control. Relevant beats clever every time.

Mastery Loop — Step 2
workflow 2 ✎
WORKFLOW 2
Check-for-Understanding Questions
The time drain. Be honest — how many times have you asked 'does everyone get it?', watched a sea of nods, and found out at the exit slip that roughly nobody got it? Asking good questions mid-lesson on the fly is hard, so we default to the question that tells us nothing.
Why bother. This builds you a stash of real check-for-understanding questions for the lesson you're teaching RIGHT NOW — the kind that catch a misconception while it's still cheap to fix, not after it's metastasized into next week's unit. No more flying blind between 'I taught it' and 'they bombed the quiz.'
The mastery angle. Catching the wrong turn at the moment it happens is the whole ballgame. Questions that range from 'can you repeat it' to 'can you actually use it' tell you the difference between kids who memorized and kids who get it — which, let's be real, is not the same crowd.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Here’s a humbling one. I taught what I thought was a clean lesson on cell transport, asked ‘everybody good?’, got the whole room nodding like bobbleheads, and felt great about myself. Exit slip? Carnage. Turns out ‘are we good?’ only measures who’s willing to make eye contact. Now I bake in actual check questions mid-lesson — the kind where a wrong answer tells me exactly which wire crossed — and I find the confusion while it’s still a five-minute fix instead of a ‘well, there goes the unit test’ situation.

The mastery angle. Here's the catch nobody mentions: a 'fun' activity that doesn't match your objective is just expensive chaos — glitter and noise, zero learning. The whole game is aiming the activity at the exact skill you're grading, so it's practice that counts and not just a vibe.

WORKFLOW 4
Exit Slip For Today's Lesson
The time drain. A good exit slip should take three minutes for them to do and thirty seconds for you to read. Writing one that actually lines up with today's objective — every single day — is more work than it looks, which is why so many of us reach for a vague 'what did you learn today?' and get back 'idk, stuff.'
Why bother. This is your daily mastery readout — a tight little slip that tells you who actually got today's objective. It's the most important data point in your whole day, because it's the thing that writes tomorrow's warm-up and your reteach. Garbage exit slip, garbage everything downstream. So we make it good, fast.
The mastery angle. This is the moment of truth in the loop. Aligned tight to the objective, it gives you a clean read on who hit mastery and who's bluffing — the data that makes every move tomorrow a sniper shot instead of a shotgun blast.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Two lessons I learned the hard way. One: a long exit slip gives you garbage data, because by question five they’re just trying to make the bell. Keep it to three questions that actually matter. Two: when the data’s good, it genuinely runs your next day for you. I had an AP Psych exit slip flag that half the room thought correlation meant causation (classic), and that single slip wrote my entire next-day opener. Short, aligned, ruthless — that’s the whole game.

Grade/subject: [grade/subject].

WORKFLOW 5
Quick Grading + Feedback That Develops Questions
The time drain. Let's name the real villain: the stack. The pile of exit slips that follows you home, sits on the kitchen table judging you, and turns into 45 minutes of 'good job!' comments that nobody reads. This is where your evenings go to die.
Why bother. This one's the hours-saver. It chews through your exit-slip results, tells you what they actually got wrong and why, and spits out targeted feedback PLUS the questions to ask next. Same-day, useful feedback that doesn't require you to give up your entire prep — finally realistic.
The mastery angle. Feedback is, no exaggeration, one of the most powerful things you do all day — but only if it's specific and lands while the lesson's still warm. This turns a pile of slips into an actual plan, which is the difference between 'measured it' and 'fixed it.' If you build only one assistant from this whole book, make it this one.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
The grading stack and I have a long, toxic history. It ate entire weekends. I’d hand feedback back so late the kids had emotionally moved on, and I’m pretty sure I wrote the literal same comment thirty times in a row until the words stopped meaning anything. This is the workflow that gave me my evenings back — not by grading for me, but by spotting the patterns fast so my feedback actually lands while the kids still care. If you build one assistant from this whole book, you already know which one I’m going to say.

WORKFLOW 6
Mini-Recap / Targeted Reteach
The time drain. The exit slips are in and — oof — a chunk of the class face-planted on one thing. Now you need a reteach by tomorrow morning that hits that ONE thing without boring the kids who nailed it. Building that at 6 a.m. is nobody's idea of a good time.
Why bother. This builds a short, surgical reteach aimed only at the thing they actually missed — not a do-over of the whole lesson while half the room mentally checks out. Respect everyone's time, fix the actual leak, move on.
The mastery angle. Mastery is about closing the specific gap, not re-covering the whole map. A mini-recap built from your exit-slip data hits the exact misconception that's gumming up the works, so tomorrow's lesson isn't built on sand.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
My favorite kind of win. A chunk of my bio kids absolutely faceplanted on a concept — I’d explained it the way I always do, the way that makes perfect sense in my head and apparently no one else’s. So I had it help me rebuild the reteach from a completely different angle, a new analogy I’d never have reached for, and watched it click for kids who’d been lost for two days. Same content, different door in. Sometimes the kids aren’t the problem; my one explanation was.

WORKFLOW 7
Tomorrow's Do Now + Extension Activities
The time drain. Now do the impossible: plan a warm-up that patches yesterday's gaps for the strugglers AND something worthwhile for the kids who already get it and are three seconds from causing problems out of boredom. That's two preps in a trench coat pretending to be one.
Why bother. This closes the loop. It builds tomorrow's Do Now straight from today's gaps, plus extensions for the kids who've already mastered it so they're challenged instead of fluorescent-light-bored. Differentiation without doubling your work — yes, really.
The mastery angle. This is what makes the loop self-running and actually differentiated. Strugglers get one more rep on the exact thing that tripped them; your fast finishers get pushed deeper instead of idling. Instruction that flexes both directions off real data — that's differentiation that isn't just a buzzword on your eval.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Real talk: differentiating both ends at once used to feel like a cruel joke. Patch the strugglers AND feed the kids who finished in four minutes and are now narrating a documentary about my stapler? With one prep? Please. But the fast-finisher chaos is real — a bored kid who already gets it will absolutely invent a new problem for you. When I started building actual extensions that pushed them instead of just ‘read ahead,’ the behavior stuff quieted down and the smart kids stopped coasting. Turns out they were acting out because I was boring them. Ouch, but fair.

Wire The Whole Loop Together
The time drain. Running these seven steps one at a time means re-explaining your whole situation to the AI over and over like you're stuck in a meeting that could've been an email. The time leaks out of the seams between steps.
Why bother. This connects Steps 1–7 into one continuous machine and turns the whole cycle into a single assistant that already knows your deal. The loop's magic is the hand-off — each thing feeding the next — and wiring it together means yesterday's data shapes today's teaching with basically zero friction.
The mastery angle. Wire it together and data-driven teaching stops being the thing you do when you're feeling ambitious and becomes your default. The system holds the context so you can spend your energy on, you know, the actual humans in the room.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Re-explaining my whole situation to the AI every single morning got old fast — it was like training a brand-new sub at 6 a.m. daily. But once I wired it together so it just KNEW my course, my unit, my kids? It started running itself. And I’ll be honest, building that first real Gem felt a little like magic — the good kind of ‘wait, I made that?’ magic that you don’t get a lot of in year-whatever of teaching. This is the one that turns ‘cool trick’ into ‘actual system.’

Attendance Tracker
The time drain. Taking attendance is thirty seconds. Noticing that Jayden's been 'mysteriously' absent every Friday for a month, cross-referencing it, and remembering who you already called home about — that's the part that quietly eats your life across a semester.
Why bother. Attendance is one of the earliest tells that a kid's in trouble; catching it early actually changes the ending.
The mastery angle. Nobody masters anything from an empty desk. Spotting the pattern early lets you reach out before the absences quietly torch a kid's grade — protecting the one thing learning absolutely requires, which is them being there.

Homework / Work Completion Tracker
The time drain. Logging who turned in what, noticing who's quietly stopped, and writing the 'hey, I noticed…' messages is a slow, invisible drain that's easy to let slide — right up until a kid's buried in a hole you didn't see them digging.
Why bother. This tracks completion, flags the patterns (the chronic missers AND the sudden drop-offs, which are usually the real emergency), and drafts the gentle nudge. Missing work is often the first visible flare that something's off — this helps you catch it while it's still a conversation, not a crisis.
The mastery angle. Practice is where the learning gets built, so skipped practice is the canary in the coal mine. Spot the pattern early and you can pull a kid back in before the gaps stack up into something a single pep talk can't fix.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Missing work is almost never about the work. Nine times out of ten, the assignment that stopped coming in was the first flare that something else was going on — home stuff, a rough patch, a kid quietly drowning. I had this one student whose completion just fell off a cliff, and catching the pattern early turned it into a check-in instead of a failing grade. The tracker isn’t about being the homework police; it’s an early-warning system for ‘this kid needs someone to notice.’

WORKFLOW 12
Parent / Family Correspondence
The time drain. Writing the family email — especially the hard one, or the one that has to land in a language you don't speak — is the task that sits in your drafts folder for four days radiating guilt. The emotional tax on these is real, and it keeps us from reaching out as much as we should.
Why bother. This drafts the message fast: good-news notes, the careful concern email, the update, plus a translation. Strong family partnership genuinely moves the needle for kids, but the time and the stomach-knot of writing keeps most of us from doing it often enough. This removes the excuse.
The mastery angle. Family in the loop is a documented win for student success — not a fluffy nice-to-have. Faster, clearer messages mean more good-news contact and earlier flares when something's wrong, both of which quietly build the conditions a kid needs to actually master your content.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Two honest things here. First, a good-news message hits harder than people think — I’ve watched a thirty-second positive note completely change how a kid shows up the next day. Worth every second. Second, the real skill isn’t just writing the email, it’s knowing WHEN to reach out — and which parent you’re dealing with. The disengaged family you’re trying to gently pull in is a totally different message than the… enthusiastically engaged one who emails you four times before lunch. This helps me hit the right tone for both without spending my whole prep period drafting and deleting.
