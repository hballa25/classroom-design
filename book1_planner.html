The AI-Powered Classroom
read me first ✎
Real Quick, Before You Hand This to Kids
The big idea. Let's be honest: your students are already using AI, and a chunk of them are using it to not think. Banning it is a losing game. So this workbook does the opposite — it puts AI to work as a tutor, a sparring partner, a coach — and rigs every project so the kid still does the actual thinking.
✍ IF YOU'RE SKEPTICAL, GOOD — I WAS TOO
My honest fear was that handing kids a chatbot meant they'd offload the thinking and learn nothing — and my first messy attempts proved I was right to worry. What fixed it wasn't banning it; it was structure. Done right, the kids don't become cheaters. They become detectives who get weirdly competitive about catching the AI being wrong. That's the whole game.
What's in every project:
Grade band & objective — match it to your students at a glance.
Purpose & Why, and How It Builds Mastery — the learning rationale.
A Free Track and Paid Track prompt — for Claude or Gemini.
From My Classroom — how I introduced it, including what went wrong first.
Build the Assignment — how to make it student-ready with a guardrail.
Extension & Follow-Up activities.
The Three Rules (give these to students):1) AI is a starting point, never the finish line. 2) If you can't explain it, you don't own it. 3) Always verify — catching its mistakes means you're thinking.

project 1 ✎
PROJECT 1
Interview an Expert (Historical or Scientific)
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Generate questions, conduct a simulated interview, then fact-check the responses against a real source.
Purpose & Why. Students often consume information passively. This project flips them into the role of interviewer and fact-checker, building source-evaluation skill while making an abstract figure feel concrete and present.
How It Builds Mastery. Students choose a historical figure or scientist and use AI to simulate an interview. The mastery move is the verification step: after the AI responds in character, students must locate one real source that confirms or contradicts a claim the AI made. This builds the single most important AI-literacy habit — treating AI output as a draft to verify, not a fact to accept. The act of writing good interview questions also forces students to identify what they actually don't know about the topic, which is itself a comprehension check.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Full disclosure: I went into the whole AI-in-my-classroom thing as a skeptic. My honest fear was that I’d hand kids a chatbot and they’d just get it to do everything while learning nothing. My first attempt was a mess — a couple kids tried to get it to spit out the whole assignment, a couple took its first answer as gospel, and a couple just sat there frozen because they had no idea what to ask. What turned it around was the rule that came out of that disaster: write your questions first, then verify what it tells you. The day I watched a kid in my Living Environment class triumphantly catch the AI inventing a fake date about Rosalind Franklin — and then go dig up the real one to prove it — I stopped being a skeptic. They weren’t cheating. They were fact-checking. That’s the assignment now.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
You are [historical figure / scientist]. Stay fully in character and answer from your own time period and knowledge. I am a student interviewing you. Answer my first question in 3–4 sentences, then wait for me to ask the next one. My first question is: [question]. After 5 questions, list 3 claims you made that I should fact-check.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Roleplay as [figure] for a student interview, staying historically accurate to what was known in their lifetime. After each answer, add a short [VERIFY THIS] tag flagging any claim a student should confirm. Maintain the persona across the whole conversation, adapt vocabulary to a [grade level] reader, and at the end produce a sourced fact-check table of every factual claim you made, marking each as Confirmed, Disputed, or Anachronistic.
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Introduce it by modeling one interview live on the projector first — let the class feed you questions so they see what a good one looks like before they’re loose on their own. Assign each student (or pair) a figure tied to your unit. Guardrail: they must write their interview questions BEFORE they open the AI, so the thinking starts with them. Turn-in: their question list, plus a short reflection on which AI answer they fact-checked and what they found — confirmed, off, or totally made up.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Have students run the SAME interview on both Claude and Gemini and compare where the two AIs disagree — disagreements are prime fact-checking targets.
Students write a one-paragraph 'correction notice' for any inaccuracy they caught, citing their source.
Turn the verified interview into a first-person monologue performance or podcast segment.

project 2 ✎
PROJECT 2
Source Showdown: AI vs. The Real Article
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Compare an AI summary of a topic to a real, teacher-vetted article and identify what the AI added, omitted, or distorted.
Purpose & Why. Students need to feel, concretely, that AI summaries are lossy and sometimes wrong. Putting the AI version next to a real source makes the gaps visible instead of theoretical.
How It Builds Mastery. The teacher supplies (or approves) one real article. Students ask the AI to summarize the same topic blind, then run a structured comparison: what did the AI include that the article didn't, what did it omit, and what did it get subtly wrong? This builds critical reading of BOTH texts at once and teaches that fluency is not accuracy — AI prose sounds confident whether or not it's right.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
The thing that makes this work is giving them the real article as the control — otherwise they have no way to know what the AI fudged. My early mistake was skipping that step and just trusting kids to ‘notice’ problems; they didn’t, because the AI’s writing SOUNDS authoritative. Once there’s a real source to check against, suddenly they can see the gaps. Fluency isn’t accuracy, and this is the project that teaches that better than any lecture I could give.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
Summarize the topic of [topic] in about 200 words for a student. Be specific and include key facts, dates, and names. Do not soften or generalize.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Summarize [topic] in 200 words. Then create a self-audit: list every factual claim you made as a numbered list, and for each one rate your confidence (High/Medium/Low) and note whether it is the kind of claim that commonly appears in unreliable sources. Flag anything you are uncertain about explicitly.
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
You supply the real article; that’s the control. Model the comparison once with the whole class so they see the AI miss something the article had. Then students run their own. Guardrail: they can’t start the comparison until they’ve read the real source first. Turn-in: a reflection naming one thing the AI added, one it left out, and one it got subtly wrong — with the evidence from the real article.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students rewrite the AI summary to fix every error they found, producing a 'corrected edition.'
Run the same prompt on Gemini and Claude; students chart which AI included more verifiable facts.
Students design a 'trust score' rubric and grade the AI summary with it.

project 3 ✎
PROJECT 3
Fact or Fabrication Detective
Grades: Elementary / Middle  ·  Objective: Identify AI 'hallucinations' by deliberately asking about obscure or invented details and checking the results.
Purpose & Why. Younger students especially assume AI 'knows everything.' This project gives them direct, memorable proof that AI can confidently make things up — inoculating them early.
How It Builds Mastery. Students ask the AI about something slightly obscure within a class topic, then verify each detail. When the AI invents a fake fact (a made-up date, a nonexistent person), students 'catch' it like detectives. The gamified framing makes a serious lesson stick: AI fluency is not the same as truth. Students keep a running 'evidence log' of confirmed facts versus fabrications.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
If you’re a skeptic like I was, start here, because it turns your biggest fear inside out. My worry was kids blindly trusting whatever the AI says — so this project makes them HUNT for the lies. The first time my Living Environment kids caught it confidently inventing a fake scientist, the room lost it. They were delighted. And then they couldn’t un-see it — they started questioning everything it told them, which is exactly the habit I wanted. The rule it cemented: never let a kid take the first AI answer as gospel. Make them prove it. Turns out kids LOVE proving the machine wrong.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
I'm learning about [topic]. Tell me 5 specific facts including names and dates. After each fact, tell me how sure you are and whether I should double-check it.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
I'm a student studying [topic]. Give me 5 detailed facts with names, dates, and numbers. Then act as your own fact-checker: re-examine each fact and tell me which ones you are confident are accurate, which might be wrong, and which you may have invented. Explain how a student could verify each one.
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
This one introduces itself — kids love catching the machine lying. Model it by asking the AI something obscure in your unit and letting the class watch it potentially invent an answer. Assign a topic, have them hunt fabrications. Turn-in: their ‘evidence log’ of confirmed facts vs. caught fabrications, plus a one-line reflection on which questions were most likely to trip the AI up and why.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Class builds a shared 'Hall of Fabrications' wall of caught AI mistakes.
Students write a kid-friendly 'How to Spot a Fake Fact' guide.
Compare how Claude and Gemini handle the same obscure question.

project 4 ✎
PROJECT 4
Debate Prep War Room
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Use AI to build the strongest possible case for BOTH sides of a debate, then evaluate which arguments actually hold up.
Purpose & Why. Strong debaters must understand the opposing view. AI can rapidly generate both sides, but students must judge argument quality — which is the real intellectual work.
How It Builds Mastery. Students assign the AI to argue both sides of a class-relevant question, generating claims and evidence for each. The mastery work is evaluation: students rank the arguments by strength, flag which 'evidence' needs verification, and identify logical weaknesses. This builds argumentation, perspective-taking, and source-skepticism simultaneously.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
What I love here is watching kids steel-man a side they hate. The guardrail that matters: every ‘fact’ the AI hands them has to get verified before it counts, because the AI will absolutely invent a convincing statistic. Once kids realize that, they start treating its claims like a defense attorney treats a witness. That skepticism is the real lesson hiding inside a debate project.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
Give me the 3 strongest arguments FOR and the 3 strongest arguments AGAINST: [debate question]. For each argument, note what evidence would be needed to support it.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Build a debate brief on [question]. Provide the 4 strongest arguments per side, each with the type of evidence required and its weakest point. Then add a 'judge's note' assessing which side currently has the stronger case and what evidence would change the outcome. Keep it at a [grade level] reading level.
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Frame it as building ammunition, not getting answers. Model generating one strong argument for a side you find weak. Assign the debate question; students build both sides. Guardrail: every ‘fact’ the AI gives must be flagged for verification before it can be used. Turn-in: their ranked arguments plus a reflection on which AI-generated argument they found weakest and why.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students hold the live debate using the AI-prepped briefs but must verify every cited 'fact' first.
Switch sides last-minute to test true understanding.
Students write a reflection on which AI-generated argument they found weakest and why.

project 5 ✎
PROJECT 5
Primary Source Decoder
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Use AI to decode difficult primary-source language, then independently verify the interpretation.
Purpose & Why. Primary sources are gold for learning but their archaic or technical language blocks many students. AI lowers the barrier without removing the thinking.
How It Builds Mastery. Students paste (teacher-approved) tricky primary-source text and ask the AI to paraphrase and explain context. The critical step: students must check the AI's interpretation against what they already know and flag anything that seems like an overreach. This keeps the cognitive work with the student while removing the decoding bottleneck.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
This one’s a lifesaver for the kids who shut down the second they see old-timey language. The AI lowers the wall so they can actually engage — but the rule I hold firm on is they have to point back to the original words to defend any interpretation. Otherwise they’re just trusting the paraphrase, and the AI’s paraphrase can quietly editorialize. Access without abandoning the thinking; that’s the balance.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
Here is a primary source from [time period]: [paste text]. Put it in plain modern English, then explain the historical context a student would need to understand it. Note any words where the meaning is uncertain.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Translate this primary source into plain modern English for a [grade level] student: [text]. Provide a side-by-side: original phrase, modern meaning, and why it mattered then. Flag any interpretation that is debated by historians and explain the debate. Do not invent context — if something is unknown, say so.
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Hand them a genuinely hard source — the point is the AI lowers the decoding wall, not the thinking. Model decoding one tricky passage together. Guardrail: after the AI paraphrases, students must point to specific words in the ORIGINAL that support the interpretation. Turn-in: their own interpretation, defended with original text, plus a reflection on anything the AI overreached on.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students annotate the ORIGINAL source by hand using insights from the AI decode.
Compare two AIs' interpretations and investigate any disagreement.
Students find a second primary source on the same event and compare perspectives.

project 6 ✎
PROJECT 6
Research Question Refiner
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Turn a broad, vague topic into a focused, researchable question with AI as a thinking partner.
Purpose & Why. Most student research fails at the first step: a question too broad to answer well. Refining the question is where research quality is won or lost.
How It Builds Mastery. Students start with a broad interest and use the AI to narrow it into a focused, researchable question through a series of refinements — but the student makes every narrowing decision. The AI suggests angles and points out where a question is too broad or too narrow; the student chooses the direction. This builds the under-taught skill of inquiry design.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Most of my kids’ research used to die at the starting line with a question so broad you could write a library about it. This fixes that, but only if the kid makes every narrowing choice — the second I let the AI ‘pick’ the question, the ownership evaporates. Watching a kid sharpen ‘something about climate’ into something they’re actually curious about is a quiet little win every time.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
My broad topic is [topic]. Help me narrow it into a focused research question. Suggest 4 different angles I could take, and for each tell me if it's too broad, too narrow, or about right. Let ME pick the direction — then help me sharpen my choice.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Help me refine [broad topic] into a strong research question. Walk me through narrowing it: suggest angles, flag scope problems, and ask what specifically interests me. Once I choose, pressure-test the question — is it answerable with evidence, appropriately scoped, and arguable? Suggest the types of sources I'd need. I make all final decisions. Level: [grade].
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Model taking one broad topic and narrowing it live, thinking aloud so they see the decisions. Students bring their own broad topic. Guardrail: the student makes every narrowing choice — the AI only suggests and flags scope. Turn-in: their ‘before and after’ question and a short reflection on what made the final version stronger.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students draft 3 sub-questions that support their main question.
Map what evidence would answer the question before researching.
Compare a 'before' and 'after' version of the question and explain the improvement.

project 7 ✎
PROJECT 7
The Revision Coach (Not the Writer)
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Submit student-written work and use AI for targeted revision feedback without letting it rewrite the piece.
Purpose & Why. Revision is where writing growth happens, yet it's the stage students most avoid. An AI coach gives instant, specific feedback so revision becomes doable and frequent.
How It Builds Mastery. Students write first, then ask the AI to act strictly as a coach — pointing out weak spots and asking guiding questions rather than rewriting. The prompt explicitly forbids the AI from writing replacement text, which keeps ownership and growth with the student. This builds metacognition about their own writing and trains them to self-edit.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
This is the one that finally sold me, because it directly killed my biggest fear: kids offloading the writing. The trick is the AI literally isn’t allowed to rewrite anything — it can only coach. My messy first run taught me to model it live, because the first time I just turned kids loose, half of them tried to get it to ‘fix’ the whole paragraph and were baffled when it asked them questions instead. Once I showed them on the projector what coaching looks like, it clicked. Watching a kid who normally writes one limp sentence actually wrestle with a revision question — that’s when I knew this wasn’t the cheating machine I’d been afraid of.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
Here is my draft: [paste]. Act as a writing coach. Do NOT rewrite it for me. Instead, point out the 3 weakest spots, ask me a question about each one to help me improve it myself, and tell me one thing I did well.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Act as my writing coach for this draft: [paste]. Never rewrite my sentences. Instead: (1) identify my 3 highest-impact revision opportunities, (2) for each, ask a Socratic question that leads me to the fix, (3) name the specific writing skill each one builds, and (4) rate the draft on thesis, evidence, and flow with one concrete next step per category. Match feedback to a [grade level] writer.
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
The whole sell to a skeptic is right here: the AI is forbidden from rewriting. Model it by submitting a weak paragraph and showing the class it coaches instead of fixes. Students write first, always. Guardrail: AI gives feedback and questions only — no replacement text. Turn-in: their original draft, their revision, and a reflection on what they changed and why.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students revise, resubmit, and ask the coach if the specific issue is now resolved.
Keep a 'revision log' tracking which weaknesses recur across assignments.
Students coach a peer's writing using the same question-based method.

PROJECT 8
Co-Write a Story (You Lead, AI Follows)
Grades: Elementary / Middle  ·  Objective: Build a creative story where the student makes every key decision and the AI only continues from the student's lead.
Purpose & Why. Reluctant writers freeze at the blank page. Collaborative momentum gets them writing, while a 'student-leads' rule guarantees the imagination stays theirs.
How It Builds Mastery. The student writes the opening and key plot decisions; the AI continues only when invited and always defers to the student's choices. This keeps creative ownership with the student while removing the paralysis of starting cold. Students practice narrative structure, dialogue, and pacing in a low-stakes, high-engagement format.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Great low-stakes first project for a nervous class, which is exactly why I use it early. The marking-their-own-sentences rule started as a management thing and turned into the actual lesson — kids could SEE their voice next to the AI’s and started preferring their own. The reluctant writers got unstuck without handing over the wheel. That’s the needle this threads.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
We're writing a story together. I am the lead author and you follow my choices. I'll write the first part and make the big decisions. You only continue when I say 'your turn,' and you must follow the direction I set. Never change my characters or plot without asking. Here's my opening: [student writes opening].
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Be my story-writing partner. Rules: I am lead author, you only write when I say 'your turn,' you write at most 3 sentences then stop, and you always honor my plot and character choices. After each of your turns, offer me 2 different directions I could take next but let ME choose. Track our characters so they stay consistent. My opening: [opening].
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Model the ‘student leads, AI follows’ rule live so they see who’s in charge. Best as a low-stakes first project. Guardrail: students mark which sentences are theirs vs. the AI’s, and they must override at least one AI suggestion. Turn-in: the story with their lines marked, plus a reflection on a moment they steered the AI away from its idea toward their own.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students highlight their sentences vs. AI's, then rewrite an AI section in their own voice.
Remove the AI entirely for the final chapter — student finishes solo.
Illustrate the story or adapt it into a comic/script.

PROJECT 9
Persuasive Letter to a Real Audience
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Draft a persuasive letter independently, then use AI to test its persuasiveness from the recipient's point of view.
Purpose & Why. Persuasion is abstract until students see how a real audience reacts. AI can role-play the recipient, giving immediate feedback on what lands and what doesn't.
How It Builds Mastery. Students write a persuasive letter (to a principal, official, or company) themselves, then ask the AI to respond as that recipient — revealing weak arguments and unaddressed objections. This builds audience awareness and argumentation. The student then revises based on the simulated pushback.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Having the AI play the recipient is the move — kids get instant, realistic pushback instead of waiting for me to grade it. My early version let kids use AI on the drafting too, and the letters came back soulless. Now it’s drafting by hand, testing with AI. The difference in how hard they fight for their argument once a ‘principal’ pushes back is night and day.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
I wrote this persuasive letter to [audience]: [paste]. Respond AS that person would. Be realistic — raise the objections they'd actually have and tell me which parts of my argument did and didn't convince you.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Read my persuasive letter to [specific audience]: [paste]. First respond in character as that recipient with their realistic reaction and objections. Then step out of character and coach me: which of my arguments were strongest, which objections I failed to address, and how to strengthen my appeal using ethos/pathos/logos — without rewriting it for me. Target a [grade level] writer.
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Students write the letter themselves; the AI only role-plays the recipient. Model one exchange so they see the AI push back. Guardrail: no AI on the actual drafting — only on testing it. Turn-in: their letter, the objections the AI raised as the recipient, and a reflection on what they revised to be more persuasive.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students revise and re-test until the simulated recipient is genuinely persuaded.
Actually send the strongest letters where appropriate.
Role-play the reverse: student becomes the recipient of a classmate's letter.

PROJECT 10
Vocabulary in the Wild
Grades: Elementary / Middle  ·  Objective: Use AI to generate rich example sentences and scenarios for new vocabulary, then produce original uses.
Purpose & Why. Memorizing definitions rarely produces real vocabulary ownership. Seeing words used in varied, vivid contexts — then using them — builds durable mastery.
How It Builds Mastery. Students feed in target words and ask the AI for multiple example sentences across different contexts, plus a short scenario using several words together. The student then writes their OWN original sentences and asks the AI to check whether the usage is correct and natural. This moves vocabulary from passive recognition to active production.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Definitions never stuck for my kids; seeing words used five different ways did. The guardrail I learned to add: the kid writes their own sentences and the AI checks them, not the other way around. The first time I had it backwards, kids just copied the AI’s examples and learned nothing. Flip it, and suddenly they’re producing the language, which is the whole point.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
Here are my vocabulary words: [words]. For each, give me 3 example sentences in different real-life situations. Then write one short paragraph that uses all the words together so I can see how they connect.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
My vocabulary words are: [words]. For each: give the kid-friendly meaning, 3 example sentences in varied contexts, a common mistake students make with it, and a memory hook. Then give me a fill-in-the-blank mini-quiz using all the words, and wait for my answers before telling me if I'm right. Reading level: [grade].
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Model how the AI gives varied example sentences, then show students writing their OWN and having the AI check usage. Guardrail: the AI checks student sentences, it doesn’t supply them. Turn-in: their original sentences using the words, plus a reflection on any usage the AI flagged and how they fixed it.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students write original sentences and have the AI check usage, not supply it.
Create illustrated word cards from the AI scenarios.
Students teach 3 words to a partner using their own examples.

PROJECT 11
Tone Transformer
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Rewrite the same message in different tones to understand audience and register, then justify the best choice.
Purpose & Why. Students often have one writing 'voice.' Seeing a single message shift across formal, casual, and persuasive registers reveals tone as a deliberate, controllable choice.
How It Builds Mastery. Students write one message, then ask the AI to show it in several tones (formal, friendly, urgent, diplomatic). The mastery work is judgment: students decide which tone fits which audience and explain why, then write their OWN best version for a target reader. This builds register awareness central to mature writing.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
This made an invisible skill visible for my kids — they genuinely didn’t realize tone was a choice they controlled until they saw one message shift five ways. The rule: the AI shows variations, but they write the final one and defend it. Watching a kid realize their text to a teacher shouldn’t sound like their text to a friend is a small thing that pays off for years.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
Here's my message: [paste]. Rewrite it in 4 tones: formal, friendly, urgent, and diplomatic. Keep the core meaning the same. After, tell me which situations each tone would be best for.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Take my message: [paste]. Show it in 4 registers (formal, casual, persuasive, diplomatic) in a comparison table with a note on the audience each suits. Then analyze what specific words and structures created each tone shift. Finally, ask me who my real audience is and recommend the best tone — but let me write the final version myself.
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Model one message shifting across tones so the lightbulb goes on. Students bring their own message. Guardrail: the AI shows variations, but the student writes the final version themselves and justifies the choice. Turn-in: the final version plus a reflection on which tone they picked, for which audience, and why.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students write the final version themselves, then justify their tone choice in 2 sentences.
Mismatch challenge: read a too-casual email to a principal and diagnose what's wrong.
Apply tone control to a real email the student needs to send.

PROJECT 12
Explain It Three Ways
Grades: Elementary / Middle / High  ·  Objective: Use AI to explain a tough concept at three difficulty levels to build layered understanding.
Purpose & Why. A concept truly understood can be explained simply. Getting three explanations — then producing a fourth — drives students toward that deep, flexible understanding.
How It Builds Mastery. Students ask the AI to explain a hard concept three ways: like they're 8, like a peer, and at an advanced level. Comparing the versions reveals the core idea beneath the jargon. The mastery step is the student writing their OWN fourth explanation in their own words, which the AI then checks for accuracy.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
I’ll admit I rolled my eyes at this one at first — felt gimmicky. Then I used it for cellular respiration, which my 7th graders treat like I’m speaking ancient Greek. The three explanations were fine, but the magic was making them write the FOURTH one themselves and having the AI check it. A kid came up with an analogy comparing the mitochondria to charging a phone overnight that was honestly better than anything in my slides. The rule I learned the hard way: the AI’s explanations are just the warm-up; the kid’s own explanation is the actual assignment. Skip that part and you’ve just got fancier note-taking.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
Explain [concept] three ways: (1) like I'm 8 years old, (2) like I'm your classmate, (3) like I'm an advanced student. Use a different analogy each time.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Explain [concept] at three levels (age 8, peer, advanced), each with a distinct analogy and a quick check-for-understanding question. Then ask me to explain it back in my own words, and evaluate my explanation for accuracy and completeness — correcting any misconception you find. Pitch level 2 at [grade].

★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced

Track which fallacy types I struggle with and give me extra practice on those. Finally, ask me to paste one of my own arguments and help me check IT for fallacies. Level: [grade].

PROJECT 27
Mock Interview Simulator
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Practice job, college, or scholarship interviews with realistic AI questioning and feedback.
Purpose & Why. Interview skill comes only from practice, which most students never get. AI offers unlimited, judgment-free reps with realistic questions and feedback.
How It Builds Mastery. Students provide the role they're interviewing for and have the AI conduct a realistic interview, then give feedback on their answers. Students can redo it as many times as needed to build confidence — something impossible with a single human mock interview. This builds communication, self-presentation, and poise.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
I was a skeptic about this being ‘real’ enough to matter — until I watched it help a kid who freezes up in front of people. The unlimited redo is the whole thing; no human mock interview lets a nervous kid try fifteen times without judgment. I model one on the projector first so they see it’s safe and a little funny when it goes sideways. The win that got me: a normally terrified kid did the practice enough times that they walked into a real scholarship interview and actually nailed it. That’s not a chatbot doing the work. That’s a kid getting reps they’d never otherwise get.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
Conduct a realistic mock interview for [job/college/scholarship]. Ask one question at a time and wait for my answer before the next. Ask follow-ups like a real interviewer. After 6 questions, give me specific feedback on my answers.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Run a realistic mock interview for [specific role/program]. Mix common and curveball questions, one at a time, with realistic follow-ups based on my answers. After each answer, give a quick rating and one improvement tip. At the end: my strengths, my top 2 growth areas, and model 'strong answer' frameworks (not scripts) for my weakest questions. Match the formality of [context].
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Model one mock interview on the projector so nervous kids see it’s safe. Guardrail: the value is the redo — students run it multiple times targeting weak spots. Turn-in: a reflection on their two biggest growth areas and how their answers improved across attempts.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students re-run the interview targeting only their weak areas.
Record answers and self-evaluate body language and delivery.
Peer mock interviews using AI-generated question banks.

PROJECT 28
Pitch Your Idea to an Investor
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Develop and pitch an original idea to an AI playing a tough but fair investor.
Purpose & Why. Pitching forces clarity, persuasion, and resilience under questioning — transferable skills for any field, made vivid by a realistic investor simulation.
How It Builds Mastery. Students develop a business, project, or invention idea and pitch it to the AI acting as a sharp investor who asks probing questions. ✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Letting the AI grill them like a real investor builds a resilience you can’t fake with a worksheet. The idea has to be theirs; the AI just pressure-tests it. Watching a kid get rattled by a hard question, regroup, and come back with a better pitch — that’s the entrepreneurial muscle this builds, and it transfers to way more than business.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
I have an idea: [idea]. Play a tough but fair investor. Let me pitch, then ask me the hard questions a real investor would — about cost, audience, competition, and feasibility. Tell me honestly whether you'd invest and why.

Act as a sharp, fair investor hearing my pitch for [idea]. After my pitch, grill me with realistic questions on market, cost, competition, and risk — one at a time. Then give a verdict (invest/pass) with reasoning, score my pitch on clarity, persuasion, and feasibility, and tell me the ONE change that would most improve it. Don't go easy on me. Level: [grade].

Students revise and re-pitch until they earn the 'investment.'

PROJECT 29
Financial Decision Lab
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Work through realistic money scenarios with AI as a financial-literacy coach.
Purpose & Why. Financial literacy is rarely taught yet universally needed. Realistic scenarios with an AI coach build decision-making skill before the stakes are real.
How It Builds Mastery. Students work through realistic financial scenarios (budgeting a first paycheck, comparing loans, saving for a goal) with the AI explaining trade-offs and consequences — but the student makes the decisions. The AI then plays out the consequences. This builds numeracy, planning, and consequential thinking in a safe sandbox.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
This teaches something school criminally underteaches, and the safe-sandbox part matters — kids make money mistakes here where the only cost is a lesson. The AI runs the consequences; the kid decides. The rule is they name the principle behind what happened. A kid realizing why a payday loan is a trap, in a simulation, beats finding out for real at 22.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
Give me a realistic financial scenario for a teen/young adult about [budgeting/saving/a big purchase]. Present the situation and options, let ME decide, then explain what would realistically happen as a result. Coach me — don't decide for me.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Be my financial-literacy coach. Create a realistic scenario about [topic] with real numbers. Present options with trade-offs, let me choose, then simulate the realistic consequences over time. Ask what I'd do differently and explain the underlying principle (interest, opportunity cost, etc.). Keep math at [grade] level and never just give me the 'right' answer.
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Model one scenario, making the decision out loud and weighing trade-offs. Guardrail: the AI runs consequences; the student decides. Turn-in: a reflection on the decision they made, what happened, and what they’d do differently — naming the principle involved.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students track a real (or realistic) monthly budget for a week.
Compare two students' decisions on the same scenario.
Research a real financial product and have the AI quiz them on it.

PROJECT 30
Professional Email Bootcamp
Grades: Middle / High  ·  Objective: Learn to write effective professional emails by drafting independently and getting AI feedback.
Purpose & Why. Professional communication is a make-or-break adult skill students get little practice with. AI feedback makes deliberate practice possible.
How It Builds Mastery. Students draft professional emails for realistic situations (requesting a recommendation, following up, addressing a problem) themselves, then get AI feedback on tone, clarity, and professionalism. ✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
Pairs perfectly with the tone project, and it’s pure real-world survival skill. Kids draft first; the AI coaches, never rewrites. The first time a kid’s too-casual email to a ‘principal’ got gently roasted by the AI, the whole class learned something I’ve been preaching for years. Some lessons land better from the machine than from me, and I’ve made peace with that.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
I need to write a professional email to [recipient] about [situation]. I'll write it first — [paste my draft]. Give me feedback on tone, clarity, and professionalism. Don't rewrite it; tell me what to fix and why so I learn.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Coach me on this professional email to [recipient] about [situation]: [paste]. Assess tone, clarity, structure, and professionalism with specific fixes (not a rewrite). Flag anything too casual or unclear, suggest a stronger subject line, and rate it 1–10. Then give me a NEW scenario to practice on. Context formality: [setting].
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Students draft first; the AI coaches tone and clarity. Model improving a too-casual email. Guardrail: AI gives feedback, not a rewrite. Turn-in: their original draft, their revision, and a reflection on what they fixed and why it matters professionally.

Students send a real (appropriate) email and reflect on the response.

Diagnose what's wrong with deliberately bad sample emails.

PROJECT 31
Day-in-the-Life Career Shadow
Grades: Elementary / Middle / High  ·  Objective: Explore a career by interviewing the AI as a professional in that field, then verify against real sources.
Purpose & Why. Career exploration is often limited to whatever jobs students happen to see. An AI 'shadow day' opens any field for exploration, building aspiration and informed planning.
How It Builds Mastery. Students choose a career and interview the AI role-playing a professional in that field about a typical day, required skills, and pathways. They then verify key claims (salary, education required) against a real source like the Bureau of Labor Statistics. This builds career awareness AND the verification habit in one project.
✍ FROM MY CLASSROOM
This opens doors for kids who don’t have professionals in their lives to ask. The non-negotiable rule: verify the AI’s claims against a real source, because it WILL oversell a salary or fudge a requirement. Watching a kid get genuinely excited about a career they’d never heard of — then fact-check it like a pro — is the exact combination of inspiration and skepticism I want them leaving my room with.
✓ FREE TRACK — Claude (free) or Gemini (free)
Roleplay as a professional [career]. I'm a student exploring this job. Tell me about a typical day, the skills I'd need, and how someone gets into this field. Answer one part at a time. At the end, list 3 facts I should verify with a real source.
★ PAID TRACK — Claude (paid) or Gemini Advanced
Roleplay as an experienced [career] for a student's career-shadow interview. Cover a realistic day, required skills, education paths, challenges, and rewards — conversationally, one topic at a time. Then step out of character and give me a verification checklist: which claims (salary, job outlook, requirements) I should confirm and exactly where to check them. Keep it honest about downsides too.
✎ BUILD THE ASSIGNMENT — introduce it & make it student-ready
Model an interview with the AI as a professional, then verifying a claim against a real source. Guardrail: students must fact-check key claims (salary, requirements) independently. Turn-in: a career profile of verified facts plus a reflection on anything the AI got wrong or oversold.
Extension & Follow-Up Activities
Students verify salary and outlook on a real labor-statistics site and report discrepancies.
Interview a real person in the field and compare to the AI's portrayal.
Create a one-page career profile from verified information.
